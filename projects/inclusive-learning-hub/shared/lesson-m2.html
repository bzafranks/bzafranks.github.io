📚 Module 2 · Grade 7 ELA

Character Development

Students trace how authors reveal character through action, dialogue, and internal thought, then apply the technique to original writing.

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Identify what a character says, does, and thinks as evidence of a character trait.
  • Distinguish between a character trait (who someone is) and a feeling (how someone feels right now).
  • Cite specific text evidence to support a claim about a character.
  • Apply the technique by revealing a character's trait through action and dialogue in original writing.

🧠 Key Vocabulary

  • Character trait — a part of who a character is (like brave, stubborn, or generous) — not just how they feel in one moment.
  • Evidence — a specific detail from the text (an action, a line of dialogue, a thought) that proves a claim.
  • Dialogue — the exact words a character says, shown in quotation marks.
  • Internal thought — what a character is thinking, even when they don't say it out loud.
  • Motivation — the reason behind what a character does.
  • Direct vs. indirect characterization — an author telling you a trait directly, versus showing it through action and speech.

👁️ Visual Examples

Telling (Direct)

"Maya was generous."

Showing (Indirect — Action)

"Maya split her last sandwich in half without being asked."

Showing (Indirect — Dialogue)

"'Here, take mine — I already ate,' Maya said, even though she hadn't."

🧑‍🏫 Teacher Instructions

Pacing: One 45-minute period for video + transcript + vocabulary + matching activity, one period for the Character Trait Web worksheet using a class novel excerpt, one period for the original-writing application.

Common misconception: Students often confuse a feeling ("she was scared") with a trait ("she is cautious") — a trait has to hold up across the whole story, not just one moment. Use a quick "does this describe them on a different day too?" check.

UDL note: The matching activity, read-aloud option, and response chooser below give students multiple means of engagement and expression without changing the grade-level standard (RL.7.3).

Accommodation guidance: For students with Chunked Assignments, complete the Trait Web one "spoke" at a time rather than all four at once. For students with Graphic Organizers, the Trait Web worksheet above is the required scaffold before independent writing.

🧭 Student Directions

  1. Watch the lesson video (or use Read-Aloud to listen to the transcript).
  2. Review the Key Vocabulary terms — especially the difference between a trait and a feeling.
  3. Complete the "Match the Evidence to the Trait" activity.
  4. Download and fill out the Character Trait Web for a character of your choice.
